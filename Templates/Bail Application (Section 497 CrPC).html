  • The doctrine of “further inquiry” involves a notional and exploratory assessment that may create doubt regarding the accused’s precise involvement in the crime [citation: 1].
  • The atrociousness, viciousness, and/or gravity of the offence are not, by themselves, sufficient grounds for the rejection of bail where the nature of the evidence produced in support of the indictment creates some doubt as to the veracity of the prosecution’s case [citation: 1].

IMPORTANT LEGAL NOTES — SECTION 497 Cr.P.C. (2026 POSITION)

  • Section 497(1) — Prohibitory Clause: Bail shall not be granted if there appear reasonable grounds for believing that the accused has committed an offence punishable with death, imprisonment for life, or imprisonment for ten years. Exceptions: women, minors, sick/infirm persons (first proviso); delay in trial beyond one/two years (third proviso) [citation: 4, 6].
  • Section 497(2) — Further Inquiry: If there are not reasonable grounds for believing that the accused has committed a non-bailable offence, but there are sufficient grounds for further inquiry into his guilt, the accused SHALL be released on bail pending such inquiry [citation: 4, 6].
  • Landmark 2026 Precedent (Ghazi Arab vs. The State, 2026 SCLR 9): The Supreme Court held that (i) further inquiry involves a notional and exploratory assessment; (ii) gravity of offence alone cannot justify rejection of bail; (iii) parity applies where roles are identical; (iv) delay in FIR can be a factor but not decisive if explained [citation: 1, 6].
  • 2026 Lahore High Court Precedent: Bail is the rule and refusal is an exception; malafide prosecution justifies bail [citation: 2].
  • Court’s Power under Section 498 Cr.P.C.: The High Court or Court of Session may direct that any person be admitted to bail, or that the bail required by a police officer or Magistrate be reduced [citation: 4].
  • Cancellation of Bail under Section 497(5) Cr.P.C.: Bail may be cancelled if the accused tampers with evidence, commits the same offence again, or fails to appear before the Court [citation: 6].
